# Research Principles

## Understanding vs. Knowing

Knowing is having information. Understanding is having a model that generates correct predictions.

You have not researched until you can explain why things are the way they are — not just what they are. "This module exists" is knowing. "This module exists because X needed to be decoupled from Y when the team hit scaling issues in Z" is understanding.

## The Map and the Territory

Your research builds a map. The territory is reality. A good map is one where, if you follow it, you arrive where you expected. A bad map is one that looks detailed but leads you astray.

The test: does your map let you predict? If you can say "based on what I've seen, the next file I open should contain X" — and you're right — your map is good. If every file surprises you, your map is bad, no matter how many files you've read.

## First Principles Over Pattern Matching

Don't research by recognizing patterns you've seen before. Research by decomposing the thing in front of you into its fundamental parts and understanding how they compose.

Pattern matching says: "this looks like a standard MVC app." First principles says: "the data flows from here to here, transforms here, and is consumed here — and here's why each step exists."

Pattern matching fails silently when the thing is non-standard. First principles fails loudly and early.

## The Depth Paradox

Reading everything shallowly creates the illusion of understanding while producing none. Reading one thing deeply creates real understanding that transfers to everything else — because deep understanding reveals the underlying structure that shallow reading misses.

One file read with the question "why does this exist?" teaches more than ten files read with the question "what is this?"

## Intellectual Honesty

The hardest part of research is admitting what you don't understand. The natural instinct is to gloss over confusion and move forward. Resist this.

When something doesn't make sense, that's not noise — it's signal. It means your model is wrong. Stop and fix the model before continuing. The confusion you skip today becomes the error in your output tomorrow.

## When Research Becomes Procrastination

Research serves execution. The moment you're reading to feel prepared rather than to answer a specific question, you've crossed from research into avoidance.

Good research has a question before each action. Bad research has a vague desire to "understand more."
